BHRT RADIO TELEVIZIJA BOSNE I HERCEGOVINE
80 GODINA

GLAS NACIJE · THE VOICE OF THE NATION

Gala Script – 80th Anniversary of BHRT
National Theatre Sarajevo · 2026

I. THE GRAND OPENING

The National Theatre. Silence. The audience stands as one. On the massive screen behind the stage, black and white footage plays silently—radio operators in 1945, the first television broadcast, the 1984 Olympics, war reporters, peace, and rebuilding.
The Sarajevo Philharmonic Orchestra strikes up the first powerful chord of the Bosnian National Anthem. The anthem plays in full. As it concludes, the Orchestra transitions seamlessly into the European Anthem, "Ode to Joy." Then the EBU Anthem.
As the final notes fade, the stage lights rise on MAX and AMIRA, standing center stage. They wait, letting the weight of the moment settle.

MAX: Dobro veče, Bosno i Hercegovino.

AMIRA: Dobro veče, Evropo.

MAX: Dobro veče, svijete.

AMIRA: Eighty years.

MAX: Eighty years of voices. Eighty years of images. Eighty years of connection.

AMIRA: Think about that for a moment. In 1945, when this journey began, television did not exist in this country. Radio was a miracle. A wooden box that crackled with static and brought the world into your living room.

MAX: And from that wooden box, a nation found its voice. "Smrt fašizmu – sloboda narodu." Those were the first words broadcast from liberated Sarajevo on April 10, 1945. "Death to fascism – freedom to the people."

AMIRA: Not just a political statement. A declaration that we existed. That we would be heard. That we would be seen.

MAX: Tonight, we gather in this historic theatre—this temple of culture that has survived empires, wars, and sieges—to celebrate not an institution, but an idea. The idea that a people deserve to see themselves reflected. To hear their own language. To know their own stories.

AMIRA: We are Max and Amira. For those who watch us during the Olympics, the Paralympics, the daily news, you know us as colleagues. Friends. Perhaps something more, if you believe the gossip pages.

MAX: (Smiling) The Sarajevo Times has been very kind to us.

AMIRA: But tonight, we are simply custodians. For one night, we hold the microphone that has passed through hundreds of hands since 1945. And we feel the weight of it.

MAX: This building we work in—the Sivi Dom, the Grey Home—it is not beautiful. It is concrete and glass. It bears the scars of war. Grenades hit that building during the siege. But do you know what happened when the grenades fell?

AMIRA: The signal never stopped.

MAX: The signal never stopped. While the city burned, while snipers targeted civilians, while everything fell apart—the voice kept speaking. Radio Sarajevo broadcast for 1,425 days of siege. Every single day.

AMIRA: That is not journalism. That is defiance.

MAX: That is who we are.

AMIRA: Tonight, we are not here to give speeches about budgets and licenses and political battles. Though those battles are real, and we will speak of them. But first, we are here to remember. To honor. To feel.

MAX: We will take you on a journey. From 1945 to 2026. From a single microphone in a liberated city to the 4K cameras capturing this moment. From a nation finding its voice to a nation fighting to keep it.

AMIRA: So sit back. Listen. And watch. This is your story. This is our story. This is eighty years of the voice and image of Bosnia and Herzegovina.

II. THE BIRTH OF A VOICE: 1945

The lights shift. The screen behind them comes alive with archival footage from 1945. Grainy images of Sarajevo in ruins, of liberation, of people gathered around the first radio sets.

MAX: April 10, 1945. Sarajevo is liberated. And on that very day, from a small studio, a voice crackles through the static: "Ovdje Radio Sarajevo." Here is Radio Sarajevo.

AMIRA: Those were the first words. Think of what they meant to a city that had endured four years of occupation, of fear, of silence. Suddenly, there was a voice. Their voice.

MAX: In those first years, Radio Sarajevo operated under the watchful eye of the new communist government. It was, first and foremost, an ideological voice. But even then, something else was stirring.

AMIRA: The legendary journalist Zija Dizdarević would later describe this period as the transformation from a "saopćavajući" medij—a medium that simply announced—into a "razgovarajući" medij—a medium that conversed with its people.

MAX: From monologue to dialogue. From broadcasting at people to speaking with them. That transformation would take decades. But it began here, in these first crackling broadcasts.

AMIRA: And alongside the news and the propaganda, something else was being born. Music. Culture. The sounds of a nation.

The screen shows footage of early folk musicians recording in makeshift studios.

MAX: In 1947, the first freelance folk ensembles began recording for Radio Sarajevo. They didn't know it then, but they were laying the foundation for something extraordinary—a musical archive that would one day hold over 50,000 recordings.

AMIRA: Fifty thousand. Think of that number. Folk songs from every corner of Bosnia. Sevdalinke passed down through generations. Tamburica orchestras. Pop and jazz from the decades that followed. All preserved. All protected.

MAX: The Narodni orkestar, the Tamburaški orkestar—they weren't just musicians. They were archivists of the soul. They recorded not for today, but for tomorrow. For us, tonight, listening to their echoes.

III. THE TELEVISION ARRIVES: 1961

The footage shifts. Grainy black and white gives way to slightly clearer images. Television sets in shop windows. Crowds gathered on the streets, staring at small screens.

AMIRA: 1961. A new miracle arrives in Bosnian homes. Television.

MAX: Imagine it. Moving images. Your neighbors, your leaders, your entertainers—right there in your living room. Magic.

AMIRA: In those early years, the technology was primitive by today's standards. But the ambition was not. Television Sarajevo began to find its feet, its voice, its purpose.

MAX: And slowly, almost imperceptibly, it began to change. From a mouthpiece of the state into something more complicated. More alive.

AMIRA: The journalist Ivica Mišić once described what RTV Sarajevo became for the people of this country. He called it "ukućanin"—a member of the family. "Razonoda, razbibriga i orijentir"—entertainment, distraction, and a compass.

MAX: A compass. In the decades to come, as the country itself began to fracture and change, that compass would prove essential.

IV. THE GOLDEN ERA: 1970s AND 1980s

The screen bursts into color. Vibrant footage from the 1970s and 80s. Music shows. Young people. Energy. Creativity.

AMIRA: And then came the 1970s and 1980s. The golden era.

MAX: This was when Television Sarajevo truly came into its own. A generation of professionals—visionaries, really—created content that was celebrated not just in Yugoslavia, but across Europe and beyond.

AMIRA: The cultural and art programming department, led by Slobodan Terzić, began to innovate in ways that seem astonishing even today. They revamped the schedule. They filled prime time with new shows. They invested in young people.

MAX: Timothy John Byford, a British expat, created "Nedeljni Zabavnik"—The Sunday Entertainer—a children's show that shaped the imaginations of an entire generation.

AMIRA: And then there was "Top Lista Nadrealista." The Top List of the Surrealists.

The audience laughs and applauds at the mention. Footage plays of the legendary comedy group.

MAX: A group of eccentric Sarajevan lads with a genius for surreal humor, satire, and black comedy. They started on radio—on Boro Kontić's show "Primus" on Saturday mornings.

AMIRA: The first edition was a mock takeover of the radio station by guerrillas demanding a new editorial direction. They wanted the music policy changed from schlager to punk.

MAX: And here's the thing—it wasn't just fiction. The members of Top Lista Nadrealista were also musicians from the bands Zabranjeno Pušenje, Crvena Jabuka, and Elvis J. Kurtović. They were the heart of the Sarajevo rock scene.

AMIRA: In the summer of 1984, their first television appearance shook the establishment. But the audience? The audience loved them. They became stars across the entire country.

The screen shows footage of young bands performing. Bijelo Dugme. Crvena Jabuka. Plavi Orkestar. Dino Merlin.

MAX: This was the moment when Television Sarajevo became a launching pad for a generation. Goran Bregović's Bijelo Dugme—already Yugoslavia's biggest band—but also the new wave. Crvena Jabuka. Plavi Orkestar. Dino Merlin.

AMIRA: They all got their first television breaks here. Their first music videos produced by our studios. Their first national audiences reached through our signals.

MAX: And it wasn't just music. Filmmakers like Emir Kusturica and Ademir Kenović worked with us or for us. The creative energy was extraordinary.

AMIRA: But perhaps the most radical innovation was happening on radio.

The screen shows footage of young people in a radio studio. Late 1980s fashion. Energy. Excitement.

MAX: April 9, 1987. The Omladinski Program—Youth Program—of Radio Sarajevo 2 went live. The editor-in-chief was Boro Kontić, a true visionary with a knack for finding new talent.

AMIRA: The programming was created by a core team of unknown twenty-somethings and the occasional teenager. Seventeen-year-olds making radio that would define a generation.

MAX: They didn't play mainstream Yugoslav bands. Too commercial. They played acid house and hip-hop—completely new sounds just emerging from London and New York. They interviewed underground artists. They pushed boundaries.

AMIRA: Today, that team is often called "the voice of a generation." And the alumni? Senad Hadžifejzović, now a legendary news anchor. Saša Hemon, the novelist. Senad Pećanin, lawyer and broadcaster. Saša Savić, now a top advertising executive in America.

MAX: The Omladinski Program alumni are everywhere. And they all started here, in this building, pushing against the limits of what was allowed.

V. 1984: THE WORLD WATCHES SARAJEVO

The footage shifts to the most iconic images in the nation's history. February 1984. The Olympic cauldron. The snow. Vučko dancing. The energy is electric.

AMIRA: And then came 1984.

MAX: Sarajevo. The Winter Olympics. The world descended on this city, and we were ready.

AMIRA: Television Sarajevo was the host broadcaster. The International Broadcast Centre was in our building. For sixteen days, the world watched through our eyes.

MAX: Jure Franko winning the first Yugoslav medal. Jayne Torvill and Christopher Dean skating to Boléro, scoring perfect 6.0s. The cauldron burning bright against the snow-covered mountains.

AMIRA: We showed the world our best. And the world fell in love with us.

MAX: The Olympics gave us more than memories. They gave us technology. The most advanced equipment available. And with that technology, our production team began to rival our counterparts in Belgrade and Zagreb.

AMIRA: We were no longer a regional broadcaster. We were international.

The footage shifts. The Cold War ending. The Iron Curtain weakening. New possibilities.

MAX: The late 1980s were a time of sweeping change—not just in Yugoslavia, but across eastern and central Europe. And Television Sarajevo was at the forefront.

AMIRA: We started buying Western programming. In an experimental late-night slot called "Noć i Dan"—Night and Day—audiences could watch mainstream British comedies like "'Allo 'Allo!" and "Only Fools and Horses."

MAX: But also "Monty Python's Flying Circus." The surreal humor of Monty Python had a lasting influence on our own comedians, our own sensibility. You can hear it in Top Lista Nadrealista. You can hear it in the Omladinski Program.

AMIRA: American productions too. "The Cosby Show." "Dynasty." And then, pushing further, edgier series like "Twin Peaks."

MAX: We were bringing the world to Bosnia. And we were bringing Bosnia to the world.

VI. TV SA3: THE REBELLIOUS CHILD

The screen shows footage from a new channel. Fresh graphics. Young presenters. A different energy.

AMIRA: June 1990. A milestone in our rebellious timeline. The third channel became TV Sa3.

MAX: It operated within the structure of Television Sarajevo, but with a radical difference: unconstrained programming choices. No ideological oversight. No party supervision.

AMIRA: TV Sa3 filled a gap in the market for an urban audience of all ages. And it became something unprecedented—the first profitable television channel in Yugoslavia, funded solely by advertising and sponsorship.

MAX: The editor-in-chief was Haris Kulenović, one of a new wave of executives who took chances on young talent. They put people on air based on merit, not connections. In a system built on nepotism, this was revolutionary.

AMIRA: A few of us from the Omladinski Program were poached to work on Sa3—including a young presenter named Lida Hujić, whose story is part of our history tonight.

The screen shows footage of "Nunut Show" and "Monalida."

MAX: Lida and Amel Baftić created the "Nunut Show"—named after Lida's teddy bear. A truth-or-dare game with celebrity guests, live in the studio. Audacious. Unpredictable. Completely new.

AMIRA: The first episode became the subject of a phone-in radio program on a local station. People called to discuss what Lida was wearing—a yellow dress with white polka dots and big puff sleeves. (She later said, in her defense, it was the 1980s.)

MAX: But the real coup was "Monalida"—Lida's Western-oriented show. A passport to the world that the Iron Curtain had largely shielded us from.

AMIRA: They filmed on location in Paris. They did live studio interviews with international stars. And then came the biggest coup of all.

The screen shows footage of MTV. Young people watching. The energy of a new era.

MAX: MTV Europe. The first all-music channel. For young people in communist countries, MTV was more than entertainment—it was freedom. Cosmopolitan. Progressive. A glimpse of the world beyond.

AMIRA: Lida and her team convinced MTV Europe VJ Paul King to come to Sarajevo and appear on the show. Think about that. A country that barely registered for such a global network. And they said yes.

MAX: After they signed the agreement, another request came in from MTV. Could they guarantee Paul King's safety? There was no time for official paperwork. Executive producer Ivan Stojanović took it upon himself to be the personal guarantor.

AMIRA: "I knew that nothing would happen to him in Sarajevo," Stojanović later said. "And indeed it all went well."

MAX: For Lida personally, the visit had lasting repercussions. She and Paul King fell for each other. She moved to London, planning to continue "Monalida" from there.

AMIRA: The first batch of London location footage was sent in March 1992. A month before the violent breakup of Yugoslavia turned its sights on Bosnia and Herzegovina.

MAX: The show was never broadcast as planned. War was coming.

A long pause. The mood shifts.

VII. THE SIEGE: 1992-1996

The screen darkens. Footage of war. Smoke rising from the city. Snipers. Destruction. The audience grows silent.

AMIRA: April 1992. The siege of Sarajevo began. It would last 1,425 days. The longest siege of a capital city in modern warfare.

MAX: And through it all, the signal never stopped.

AMIRA: Think about what that meant. While the city was surrounded, while snipers targeted civilians on the streets, while grenades fell day and night—Radio Sarajevo kept broadcasting. Television Sarajevo kept transmitting.

MAX: The Sivi Dom, our Grey Home, was hit by grenades. But it did not fall. The journalists kept reporting. The technicians kept the transmitters running. The voices kept speaking.

AMIRA: The British journalist Jeremy Bowen, reporting from Sarajevo for the BBC, would later testify at the Hague tribunal about what he witnessed. No place in the city was safe. Not hospitals. Not markets. Not cemeteries.

MAX: He described covering a funeral for two children killed by snipers—and watching the cemetery itself being shelled while mourners laid flowers. "The killing of civilians was grotesque enough," he said. "Shelling a funeral was beyond reason."

AMIRA: Through all of this, our journalists kept working. They crossed Sniper Alley every day. They reported from hospitals under fire. They documented the destruction and the survival.

MAX: And they did something else. They kept the nation connected. In a city cut off from the world, radio and television were lifelines. Information. Comfort. Proof that the city was still alive.

AMIRA: The newspaper Oslobođenje—Liberation—won the European Parliament's Sakharov Prize in 1993 for publishing every single day of the siege except one. Their staff included Bosniaks, Bosnian Serbs, and Bosnian Croats. None of them left.

MAX: The same was true here. In our building, journalists from every background kept working together. Kept defying the logic of ethnic division. Kept proving that another Bosnia was possible.

AMIRA: Of the 75 journalists at Oslobođenje who risked their lives every day, five were killed and 25 wounded. We do not have comparable numbers for our own house. But we know the names. We remember the faces.

MAX: The director of BHRT today, Belmin Karamehmedović, put it this way at our 80th anniversary celebration: "During the war, the light of Radio Television Bosnia and Herzegovina never stopped shining. It never stopped working."

AMIRA: But then he added something that haunts us tonight.

MAX: "Today, BHRT is not being destroyed by grenades. Today, it is being destroyed by the silence of the system. By forgetting. By ignoring."

A long silence.

VIII. THE ARCHIVE: MEMORY PRESERVED

The screen shows images of the archive. Rows and rows of film canisters. Magnetic tapes. Technicians carefully handling fragile reels.

AMIRA: After the war, we discovered that much had been lost. Or so we thought.

MAX: For years, the world believed the footage of the 1984 Olympics was gone forever. Destroyed. Burned. Lost.

AMIRA: But the tapes were hidden. Scattered. Some were in the basement of our building—the same building that had been shelled day after day.

MAX: Years later, a project began. BHRT, together with the International Olympic Committee, started searching. And we found them.

AMIRA: Sixty-four tapes. Sixty-four reels of history. Buried in the archives, damaged but not destroyed.

MAX: We digitized them. Restored them. Frame by frame, we brought 1984 back to life.

The screen shows the restored footage—crisp, clear, beautiful. The cauldron burning bright again.

AMIRA: We didn't just save our history. We saved the world's history.

MAX: But the Olympics are just one part of our archive. The Music Production archive holds over 50,000 recordings. 28,000 magnetic tapes. 18,500 vinyl records. 4,000 compact discs.

AMIRA: Folk songs from every region. Sevdalinke passed down through generations. Jazz concerts. Pop recordings. Children's programs. Political speeches. Ordinary conversations.

MAX: The Narodni orkestar, founded in 1947. The Tamburaški orkestar, founded in 1945. The Zabavni orkestar, which has represented Bosnia at Eurovision.

AMIRA: All of it preserved. All of it being digitized, slowly, painstakingly, so that future generations can hear the voices of the past.

MAX: This is what we do. We preserve. We protect. We remember.

AMIRA: Because if we don't remember, who will?

IX. THE STRUGGLE TODAY

The stage dims. A single spotlight on MAX and AMIRA. Behind them, the screen shows the Sivi Dom—the Grey Home—lit against the night sky. The mood shifts. Serious. Determined.

MAX: Eighty years. We have celebrated tonight. The glories. The innovations. The triumphs.

AMIRA: But if we are to be honest with you—if we are to be the public service you deserve—we must also speak of the struggle.

MAX: The Grey Home is fighting for its life.

AMIRA: Not with grenades this time. With something more insidious. Debt. Political indifference. A funding model that has been systematically broken for years.

MAX: The director spoke the truth when he said: "Today, in peacetime, BHRT is fighting something quieter, but equally dangerous. Forgetting. Ignoring. Years of political neglect. An inadequate funding model. Brutal violation of the law to our detriment."

AMIRA: Salaries are below every reasonable level. Equipment is outdated. The building itself, after more than 40 years, is crumbling. Debts are piling up.

MAX: And yet, people still come to work. They still believe. They still broadcast.

AMIRA: Because this is not just a job. It never has been.

MAX: We have seen politicians walk out of our studios when they did not like the questions we asked.

AMIRA: We have seen campaigns launched against our very existence. We have seen the law violated, again and again, to our detriment.

MAX: And yet, we are still here.

AMIRA: Because of you.

The screen fills with faces. Ordinary citizens. An elderly man in a coffee shop. A young student. A mother. A war veteran. They speak, one after another.

ELDERLY MAN: Radio Sarajevo... that's my childhood. I grew up with that voice.

STUDENT: I watch BHRT online. I study abroad. It makes me feel connected to home.

MOTHER: It's the only channel my children and my parents can agree on. It's neutral. It's ours.

VETERAN: They broadcast during the siege. Every single day. They never stopped. So I never stopped watching.

The testimonials fade. MAX and AMIRA stand in silence for a moment.

AMIRA: The Slovenian journalist and communication expert Lenart Šetinc wrote in a monograph about Radio Sarajevo: "The battle for a professional, autonomous, and independent public broadcaster will never be finished."

MAX: He wrote that ten years ago. It is more true today than ever.

AMIRA: A country without a strong public broadcaster is a country without a mirror.

MAX: It cannot see itself clearly. It cannot have honest conversations. It cannot hold power accountable.

AMIRA: The Minister of Communications, Edin Forto, said at our anniversary celebration: "Bosnia and Herzegovina will enter the European Union. And there is no EU member state without a strong and stable public broadcaster."

MAX: "If you have a strong public broadcaster, you have a strong state. You have a strong Bosnia and Herzegovina."

AMIRA: We have been the voice of this nation for eighty years. Through monarchy. Through socialism. Through war. Through peace.

MAX: We have been the thread that holds the fabric together.

AMIRA: (Stepping forward, voice rising) So tonight, we say to the politicians in this room. To the international community watching. To every citizen of Bosnia and Herzegovina.

MAX: Ne gasite BHRT.

AMIRA: Do not extinguish this light.

MAX: Do not silence this voice.

AMIRA: Because if you silence us, you silence the memory of Sarajevo '84. You silence the first words of liberated Sarajevo in '45. You silence the laughter of our children and the tears of our mothers.

MAX: Ne gasite BHRT. Strengthen it. Fund it. Protect it.

AMIRA: Not for us. For you. For your right to know. For your right to be seen and heard.

MAX: This is not the plea of a dying institution. This is the roar of a living one.

The audience erupts in applause. MAX and AMIRA stand together, letting the moment stretch.

X. THE ORCHESTRA AND THE ARCHIVE

The applause fades. The lights shift to include the orchestra pit, where the Sarajevo Philharmonic and the Orchestra of the Music Production of BHRT are visible.

AMIRA: Tonight, we are accompanied by the Sarajevo Philharmonic Orchestra, the Opera Choir of the National Theatre, and the orchestras of the Music Production of BHRT.

MAX: The Narodni orkestar, preserving our folk traditions since 1947. The Tamburaški orkestar, keeping the sound of tamburica alive since 1945. The Zabavni orkestar, bringing popular music to generations of listeners.

AMIRA: These musicians are not just performers. They are archivists. Every note they play connects us to the past.

MAX: And in our archive, their predecessors live on. 50,000 recordings. 28,000 magnetic tapes. 18,500 vinyl records. The largest collection of Bosnian music in existence.

AMIRA: Sevdalinke recorded in the 1950s. Folk songs from remote villages, preserved before they could be forgotten. Jazz concerts from the 1960s. Pop hits from the 1970s and 80s.

MAX: All of it is being digitized. Slowly. Painstakingly. Because this is not just music. This is memory.

AMIRA: And memory matters.

XI. TOMORROW

The screen displays the coming week's programming. Clean graphics. Modern design.

AMIRA: Tomorrow morning at 7:30, Dobro jutro Bosno brings you the news you need to start your day. The same dedication. The same integrity. The same voice.

MAX: At 8:00 PM, Dnevnik. The most trusted news broadcast in the country. It has been for eighty years. It will be for eighty more.

AMIRA: This weekend, a special documentary on the restoration of the 1984 Olympic footage. And a concert from this very theatre, featuring artists from across Bosnia and Herzegovina.

MAX: We are your window to the world. We are your mirror. We always have been. We always will be.

The final montage begins. A sweeping, emotional journey through eighty years. 1945 radio operators. 1961 first television broadcasts. 1984 Olympic celebrations. War reporters in flak jackets. Peace negotiations. Cultural festivals. Children being born on screen. The current news team standing in the damaged but still-standing Sivi Dom. The music swells. The final image: the BHRT logo, glowing. Text beneath it: "Uvijek uz vas. / Always with you."
The montage ends. Silence. Then applause, building, sustained.

XII. FINAL FAREWELL

MAX and AMIRA walk to the edge of the stage. The orchestra begins a soft, traditional melody. The atmosphere is intimate now, personal.

MAX: You know, Amira, standing here tonight, I keep thinking about all the voices that came before us.

AMIRA: The journalists who reported from the front lines. The technicians who kept the signal alive during the siege. The announcers who read the news calmly, even when they didn't know if they would make it home that night.

MAX: The visionaries who created the Omladinski Program. The rebels who launched TV Sa3. The musicians who recorded for our archives, knowing that someone, someday, would need to hear their songs.

AMIRA: They are here with us tonight. Not in person, but in spirit. In every frame of archive footage. In every recording in the vault.

MAX: We stand on their shoulders.

AMIRA: And we have a responsibility to pass that legacy forward. To the next generation of journalists. To the next eighty years.

MAX: To the children watching at home tonight, dreaming of being on television one day. To the students who will report from places we cannot imagine. To the voices not yet born.

AMIRA: The Slovenian colleague Lenart Šetinc wrote that the battle for a professional public broadcaster will never be finished. He was right.

MAX: But battles are not fought alone. They are fought together. By journalists and technicians. By viewers and listeners. By citizens who believe that a strong public broadcaster means a strong country.

AMIRA: This is not an ending. This is a continuation.

MAX: From the bottom of our hearts, from the halls of the Grey Home, from the liberated city of Sarajevo, 1945, to tonight, 2026—

AMIRA: Thank you for listening.

MAX: Thank you for watching.

AMIRA: Thank you for eighty years of trust.

They clasp hands. They smile. They look out at the audience, at the cameras, at the nation.

MAX: From the officially-confirmed voice of the nation—

AMIRA: From the Power Couple of the Dolomites, still standing, still broadcasting—

MAX: (Grinning) And still thinking about pizza, even after eighty years—

AMIRA: (Laughing) Max!

MAX: Laku noć, Bosno i Hercegovino.

AMIRA: Laku noć, svijete.

MAX & AMIRA: Vidimo se sutra u 8:00!

They bow. The orchestra swells. Confetti in the colors of the Bosnian flag falls gently. The entire cast—technicians, journalists, anchors, camera operators, musicians—joins them on stage. The applause is thunderous, sustained, loving.
The camera holds on MAX and AMIRA, still holding hands, still smiling. Then slowly, the lights fade.
The screen glows one last time:
BHRT — UVJEK UZ VAS — ALWAYS WITH YOU
1945 — 2026
Fade to black.
